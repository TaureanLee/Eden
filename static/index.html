Connecting your headset starts simple, gets deeper

Pick your headset from the list, fill in any connection details it asks for, and press connect — Eden talks to dozens of EEG devices through the BrainFlow library, so you no longer need the command line. If you do not own a headset yet, choose Synthetic to explore Eden with a realistic simulated brain. Most Bluetooth headsets such as the Unicorn or Muse need to be paired with your computer first; wired boards like OpenBCI Cyton ask for the serial port of their USB dongle, and WiFi boards ask for an IP address. If a connection fails, Eden shows you the exact error so you can fix the pairing or port and try again without restarting anything.

What am I looking at? starts simple, gets deeper

Your brain hums with a slow rhythm called theta and a fast one called gamma, and this number is simply how many fast gamma waves ride on top of each slow theta wave. When those fast bursts line up neatly on the slow rhythm, your brain is packaging information efficiently, which is a pattern tied to focus and memory, and a ratio in the neighborhood of ten tends to sit in a healthy sweet spot. Strictly speaking, what you see here is a peak-frequency ratio — the gamma peak divided by the theta peak — so it is only a quick proxy: it tells you the two rhythms are present in a good proportion, but it does not by itself prove that the gamma is genuinely nested on the theta phase. That stronger claim — true phase–amplitude coupling — is measured separately and more rigorously in the PAC panel below.

What criticality means starts simple, gets deeper

At its simplest, this shows whether your brain is running slow and sleepy, fast and wired, or balanced right in between, and that balanced middle is the “in the zone” sweet spot. Brains tend to handle information best when they sit near that balance point, called near-critical: lean too far one way and you are under-aroused, too far the other and you are over-aroused, and the marker simply slides along that line to show where you currently are. Underneath, the position is built from three independent clues — the slope of your background brain activity (the aperiodic 1/f exponent) read as a tug-of-war between excitation and inhibition, the balance of fast rhythms against the relaxed alpha rhythm read as arousal, and long-range temporal correlations that tend to peak right at the critical point. Crucially, none of these clues use the PAC result at all, so the two readouts stay completely separate and any relationship you happen to notice between coupling and criticality is something you genuinely observed rather than something the app wired together. Because all of these are indirect scalp markers rather than a direct readout of the cortex, a personal baseline over time makes them far more meaningful for you.

    Reading the per-channel bandpower starts simple, gets deeper

    Each cell shows how much power one electrode is putting into one frequency band, measured in decibel-microvolts (dBµV) just like a dedicated bandpower meter, with a small bar so you can compare channels at a glance. The brightest cell in each channel's column is that channel's dominant rhythm, and the big label up top is the dominant band across your whole head. Splitting beta into low, mid, and high lets you see the difference between relaxed alertness and tense, high-strung focus. Channels you remove on the head map, or that Eden flags as bad, are dimmed so a noisy electrode never misleads you. This view refreshes many times a second, independently of the slower, more rigorous PAC and criticality panels, so it stays as snappy as you move and think.

    How PAC works starts simple, gets deeper

    PAC asks a simple question: do the fast gamma waves keep showing up at the same point on each slow theta wave, like fireworks that always pop right at the top of a Ferris wheel’s turn? To answer it honestly, the app looks at every electrode on its own and never blends them together, because averaging channels first can invent coupling that was not really there. Before trusting any channel it checks that a real theta rhythm is actually present, rising above the background noise, and if it is not, the channel plainly reports “θ unreliable” and is skipped rather than guessed at. When theta is solid, the coupling strength itself is the Tort modulation index, a measure of how unevenly the gamma is distributed across the theta cycle, and that raw number is then z-scored against a surrogate built by time-shifting the data so that anything explainable by chance is subtracted away. Finally, because a sharp, non-sinusoidal theta wave can mathematically masquerade as gamma, a “harmonic risk” flag warns you when the apparent coupling might be that kind of waveform artifact instead of a true, independent gamma oscillator.
